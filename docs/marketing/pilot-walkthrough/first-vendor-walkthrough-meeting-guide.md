# First Vendor Walkthrough Meeting Guide

**Document type:** meeting guidance / sales-discovery operating note  
**Context:** first scheduled walkthrough with an interested casino/vendor prospect  
**Purpose:** structure the meeting so it uncovers operational pain, validates product fit, and avoids turning the walkthrough into a disconnected feature demo.

---

## 1. Core Meeting Posture

The walkthrough should not be treated as a canned product demo.

It should be a guided operational discovery conversation that uses the product as proof.

The goal is to understand the vendor's current floor workflow, the pain created by their legacy player tracking system, and whether the product maps to a real replacement need.

The meeting should feel like:

> “Let’s understand your operation first, then show how the system handles those exact moments.”

Not:

> “Let me show you every feature we built.”

The prospect already mentioned dissatisfaction with current legacy player tracking software. That is the opening thread. Pull on it carefully.

---

## 2. Recommended Meeting Frame

Open with a practical framing statement:

> “I’d like to keep this practical. Rather than walk through a canned demo, I want to understand how your floor currently operates, where the existing player tracking system slows you down, and then show the parts of d3lt that map directly to those problems.”

This does three useful things:

1. It signals that the meeting is about their operation, not your ego.
2. It gives permission to discuss pain before product.
3. It positions the walkthrough as tailored, not generic.

---

## 3. Suggested Agenda

**Recommended length:** 45–60 minutes

| Time | Segment | Purpose |
|---:|---|---|
| 0–3 min | Set frame | Establish practical, diagnostic posture |
| 3–20 min | Discovery | Understand workflow, pain, current system limits |
| 20–25 min | Reflect pain back | Confirm what matters in their words |
| 25–45 min | Targeted walkthrough | Show only product areas tied to stated pain |
| 45–55 min | Fit discussion | Identify gaps, must-haves, decision path |
| 55–60 min | Next step | Agree on follow-up, pilot, or deeper evaluation |

Do not rush the discovery section. The buyer’s own words are the most valuable material in the meeting.

---

## 4. Discovery Section

### 4.1 Current Floor Workflow

Ask these first. They reveal the actual operating environment.

- How does a player get rated today?
- Who opens, updates, and closes a rating or session?
- What happens during a busy shift when several tables need attention?
- Where do supervisors lose visibility?
- What still happens through paper, spreadsheets, calls, radio, or memory?
- What information has to be reconstructed after the fact?
- How does shift handoff currently work?
- What does management wish they knew while the floor is still active?

### 4.2 Legacy Player Tracking Pain

The prospect already volunteered that the legacy system is unsatisfactory. Make them define what that means.

- What specifically frustrates you about the current player tracking software?
- Is the problem speed, usability, reporting, visibility, staff adoption, or something else?
- What does the current system make harder than it should be?
- Where do people work around the system instead of using it properly?
- Which role feels the pain most: dealers, floor supervisors, pit boss, shift manager, GM, compliance, or finance?
- What information do you wish the system surfaced during the shift rather than after the shift?
- What causes the most rework?

### 4.3 Operational Consequence

Pain only matters commercially when it has consequence.

- What does this cost you operationally?
- Does it affect rating accuracy?
- Does it affect comp decisions?
- Does it affect cash accountability?
- Does it affect audit or compliance review?
- Does it create disputes or uncertainty after the shift?
- Where do mistakes usually show up: during the shift, after close, during review, or during compliance checks?

### 4.4 Replacement Pressure

Determine whether this is curiosity or a real buying window.

- Are you actively looking to replace the current system, or mainly exploring options?
- Is there a contract renewal, vendor issue, or operational trigger behind the timing?
- Who would need to be involved in a replacement decision?
- What would a credible pilot or evaluation need to prove?
- What would block adoption even if the product looked promising?

---

## 5. Pain Reflection Bridge

Before opening the product, summarize their pain back to them.

Use their own words as much as possible.

Example:

> “So what I’m hearing is that the issue is not just that the current system is old. It’s that floor activity, player sessions, cash movement, and review context do not stay connected cleanly enough during real operations.”

Then confirm:

> “Is that the right read, or am I missing the sharper pain?”

This is the bridge from discovery to walkthrough.

If they correct you, that is useful. They are sharpening the sales case for you.

---

## 6. Walkthrough Structure

The walkthrough should follow the operational flow, not the application menu.

Recommended sequence:

```text
Floor activity
→ player/session tracking
→ cash/accountability context
→ review/audit trail
→ operational intelligence
```

Each product section should be introduced as an answer to a pain they mentioned.

Use this pattern repeatedly:

> “You mentioned [pain]. Here is how this workflow handles that moment.”

Avoid:

> “Another feature we have is...”

That phrasing turns the product into a module catalog.

---

## 7. Product Areas to Show

### 7.1 Floor Activity

Show how the system gives visibility into live table and session activity.

Buyer question answered:

> “Can this help us understand what is happening on the floor right now?”

Relevant proof points:

- active table/session state
- player check-in or session flow
- staff/table context
- shift visibility
- supervisor review posture

### 7.2 Player and Session Tracking

Show rated play and session continuity.

Buyer question answered:

> “Can this replace or improve the operational core of player tracking?”

Relevant proof points:

- rating/session creation
- active session updates
- session close or review
- continuity across table activity
- reduced reconstruction after the fact

### 7.3 Cash and Accountability Context

Show financial activity only as operational visibility.

Buyer question answered:

> “Can leadership see cash movement and attribution clearly enough to review the shift?”

Relevant proof points:

- buy-ins
- cash-outs where applicable
- fills / credits as operational context
- voids or adjustments if relevant
- attribution to table, staff, player, session, and gaming day

Important boundary:

Do **not** imply final accounting authority, settlement truth, or reconciliation. The product can show operational financial visibility and accountability. It should not claim to be the source of final drop, count-room truth, or full custody reconstruction.

Use language like:

> “This gives you an operational record of what the system observed and who it was tied to.”

Avoid language like:

> “This gives you the final true drop.”

### 7.4 Review and Audit Trail

Show how a supervisor can review what happened without reconstructing the shift from fragments.

Buyer question answered:

> “Can this record hold up during review?”

Relevant proof points:

- staff attribution
- player/session linkage
- table linkage
- gaming-day context
- operational history
- reviewable event trail
- discrepancy notes or correction trail if available

### 7.5 Operational Intelligence

Show insight as downstream of captured floor activity.

Buyer question answered:

> “Once the floor record is connected, what can management understand?”

Relevant proof points:

- shift-level visibility
- table activity patterns
- exception visibility
- operational summaries
- insight derived from the captured workflow

Keep this grounded. Do not turn it into generic BI language.

---

## 8. Questions to Answer Before the Meeting Ends

By the end of the walkthrough, the following should be clear.

### 8.1 Operational Fit

- Which current workflow is most painful?
- Which part of the shown product matched that pain best?
- Which workflow did they expect to see that was not shown?
- Which staff roles would need to use this daily?
- What would need to be faster, simpler, or clearer for adoption?

### 8.2 Commercial Fit

- Are they evaluating replacement, augmentation, or general curiosity?
- Who owns the decision?
- Who else needs to see the product?
- What timeline or trigger is driving the conversation?
- What would a pilot need to prove?

### 8.3 Product Gap

- What did they expect that was missing?
- What current-system behavior is non-negotiable?
- What terminology did they use differently from the product language?
- What would make them hesitate?
- What would make them say, “Yes, this is worth piloting”?

---

## 9. Closing Prompts

Do not end with:

> “What did you think?”

That is too soft and invites politeness.

Use a sharper fit question:

> “Based on what you saw, where does this line up with the pain you’re feeling in the current system, and where would it need to go further before you’d consider it a serious replacement candidate?”

Follow with:

> “Who else would need to be involved in that evaluation?”

Then:

> “Would the right next step be a deeper workflow walkthrough, a pilot discussion, or a technical fit review?”

The goal is to leave with a concrete next step, not warm applause.

---

## 10. Anti-Patterns to Avoid

### 10.1 Do Not Lead With Architecture

Avoid buyer-facing terms such as:

- transactional outbox
- projection layer
- event propagation
- provenance architecture
- semantic authority
- bounded context
- telemetry pipeline
- canonical model
- infrastructure substrate

Use operational terms instead:

- floor activity
- shift visibility
- session continuity
- cash accountability
- staff attribution
- gaming day
- review trail
- operational history
- threshold visibility
- supervisor review

### 10.2 Do Not Over-Demo

Showing too much can weaken the meeting.

If the prospect has not named a pain, do not spend much time on the related surface.

The product should appear coherent, not sprawling.

### 10.3 Do Not Overclaim Financial Authority

Safe claims:

- operational cash visibility
- reviewable financial activity
- attributed buy-ins / cash-outs
- staff and table accountability
- audit-supporting record
- observed system activity

Unsafe claims:

- final accounting truth
- authoritative drop
- full reconciliation
- settlement authority
- complete custody reconstruction
- variance resolution

### 10.4 Do Not Sell Against Legacy Too Aggressively

The vendor already dislikes the legacy system. Let them say why.

Do not sound like:

> “Legacy systems are garbage.”

Sound like:

> “A lot of older systems were built around record entry, not live operational visibility. The question is where that creates friction in your floor process.”

---

## 11. Meeting Success Criteria

The meeting is successful if the following are true:

- The prospect clearly explains current operational pain.
- You understand whether the pain is strong enough to drive replacement.
- The walkthrough is tailored to the pain they named.
- The prospect sees the product as one operational system, not a feature pile.
- You identify the decision-maker and next evaluation step.
- You leave with at least one concrete follow-up action.

The meeting is not successful merely because the product looked impressive.

The meeting is successful if the prospect can say:

> “This addresses a real operational problem we have, and here is what we need to validate next.”
