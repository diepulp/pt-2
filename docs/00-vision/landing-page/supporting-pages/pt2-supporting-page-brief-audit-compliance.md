# PT-2 Supporting Page Brief — Audit Compliance

**Date:** 2026-04-10  
**Purpose:** Define the content direction for the supporting page **Audit Compliance**.

This artifact exists to keep the page grounded in implemented system reality and to prevent it from inflating into a vague trust page, a generic security page, or a full compliance manual dumped onto a marketing surface.

---

## 1. Role of This Page

The supporting-page architecture expands one operating loop at a time.

**Audit Compliance** is the page that explains how the system preserves operational records in a form that remains attributable, traceable, and reviewable later.

Its job is to answer:

> How does the system make operational records hold up when someone has to review them after the fact?

This page should not become a sermon about governance.
It should remain tied to real floor operations and reviewable history.

---

## 2. Core Promise

**Records that hold up under review.**

This is the page’s central claim.

The page should frame Audit Compliance as the operating loop that connects:

- threshold awareness during the gaming day
- permanent records
- staff attribution
- audit notes
- printable logs
- reviewable aggregates
- linked correction history
- player/event timeline context where needed

The strength of the page is record integrity.

Not vague “trust.”  
Not generic security language.  
Not paperwork theater.

---

## 3. Domain Boundary

Audit Compliance, in system terms, is primarily composed of:

- MTL threshold tracking
- CTR alerts
- compliance dashboard
- per-patron daily aggregates
- audit notes
- printable compliance records
- permanent financial records
- permanent loyalty ledger as supporting proof of non-destructive history
- staff attribution on actions
- void / reversal audit trail
- gaming-day review context
- player timeline where it supports review and investigation
- exclusion and identity-verification context only where it strengthens reviewability

If a section does not strengthen one of those ideas, it likely does not belong here.

---

## 4. What the Page Is Not

This page is **not** primarily about:

- general floor oversight metrics
- buy-in / cash-out operation detail as the main story
- Player 360 as a profile showcase
- setup, onboarding, or import
- loyalty marketing mechanics
- generic security or RBAC explanation
- philosophical “trust” language detached from actual records
- every trust bullet from the homepage stretched into its own mini-essay

Those can be referenced when necessary, but they are not the subject of this page.

---

## 5. Audience

Primary audience:

- pit managers
- floor supervisors
- administrators
- stakeholders who need to review or defend the operational record

Secondary audience:

- operators evaluating whether the system can preserve enough context to survive audit or internal review

This page should feel practical, review-oriented, and calm.

Not paranoid.  
Not legalistic.  
Not inflated.

---

## 6. Page Narrative

The page should follow a simple review-oriented sequence:

1. Surface threshold risk while operations are live
2. Capture records in a permanent form
3. Preserve attribution and correction history
4. Add notes and context where needed
5. Review, navigate, and print later without reconstructing the day from memory

This is the correct storytelling mode.

Not “here is our compliance philosophy.”  
Not “here are ten controls in boxes.”  
Not “look how regulated we sound.”

---

## 7. Primary Content Pillars

## 7.1 Surface Threshold Risk While the Day Is Still Live

This is the opening block.

The system currently supports:

- MTL threshold tracking
- CTR threshold alerts
- progressive awareness during the gaming day

This section should make clear that compliance does not begin only when someone opens a report later.
It begins when activity accumulates during operations.

### Page purpose of this pillar

To establish that the system helps surface risk while work is happening, not only after the day is closed.

---

## 7.2 Keep History Permanent

This is one of the strongest page pillars.

The system’s business rules explicitly preserve history rather than rewriting it:

- financial records are permanent
- loyalty ledger records are permanent
- corrections create new entries
- reversals remain part of the record

This section should be treated as central, not decorative.

### Page purpose of this pillar

To show that reviewable history exists because the system keeps changes linked rather than overwriting the past.

---

## 7.3 Attach Authorship and Context

A record is weak if no one can tell who created it, changed it, or annotated it.

The system supports:

- staff attribution on actions
- audit notes
- void / reversal trail
- linked correction context

This section should explain that compliance review depends on authorship and surrounding context, not just raw values in a table.

### Page purpose of this pillar

To show why the record remains defensible when someone asks who did what and why.

---

## 7.4 Make Review Practical

The page should not stop at “we capture records.”

It must show that the system helps users review them.

The system supports:

- compliance dashboard
- per-patron daily aggregates
- gaming-day navigation
- printable compliance records

This section should present the system as a review surface, not merely a data sink.

### Page purpose of this pillar

To show that captured records can actually be navigated, understood, and used during internal review or formal inspection.

---

## 7.5 Tie Review Back to the Player/Event Timeline

Compliance review is rarely about one isolated row.

Relevant context includes:

- linked activity over the gaming day
- player timeline context
- related financial and compliance events
- audit notes attached to the event history

This should be framed as supporting context, not as a second Player 360 page.

### Page purpose of this pillar

To show that the system preserves the surrounding sequence of events needed to understand what actually happened.

- calm
- specific
- integrity-focused

---

## 12. Success Criteria

The Audit Compliance page is successful if:

1. it is understandable in under 15 seconds
2. it feels tied to real operational records, not abstract policy language
3. it clearly centers threshold awareness, permanent history, attribution, and review
4. it shows why the system remains defensible later without turning into a lecture
5. it stays bounded enough that neighboring pages still matter

---

## 13. Final Summary

**Audit Compliance** should establish one clean idea:

> The system captures operational records in a form that remains attributable, traceable, and reviewable later, so the day can be inspected without rewriting history or guessing at what happened.

That is enough.

Do not let the page swell into a generic trust page with compliance vocabulary sprayed on top.
