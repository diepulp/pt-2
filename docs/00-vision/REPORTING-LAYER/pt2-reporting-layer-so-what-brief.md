# PT-2 Reporting Layer — The Concrete “So What?”

**Purpose:** clarify why the reporting layer matters commercially and operationally, beyond the existence of the underlying features.

## Executive Answer

The reporting layer is not another dashboard. It is the mechanism that converts PT-2 from a system that *records floor activity* into a system that *explains the gaming day, proves what happened, and tells management what requires action*.

That is the answer to the vendor question: **“So what?”**

## The Core Claim

PT-2 already captures the operational and evidentiary substrate:

- per-table financial activity
- session and rating activity
- anomaly alerts
- compliance triggers
- audit chain correlation
- baseline provenance
- loyalty liability

Without a reporting layer, those truths remain distributed across multiple operational surfaces.

With a reporting layer, they become a **single management artifact** that answers:

1. What did the floor produce?
2. How trustworthy is that picture?
3. What was abnormal?
4. What requires follow-up?
5. Can this be defended to management, finance, audit, or regulators?

## What the Reporting Layer Actually Does

The reporting layer assembles existing signals into a coherent gaming-day narrative.

It should produce a shift-ready and gaming-day-ready report that consolidates:

- table financial summary
- hold / win-loss / drop / fills / credits
- rating activity and coverage
- unrated and ghost exposure
- anomaly alerts and acknowledgment state
- MTL / CTR trigger summary
- opening baseline provenance quality
- audit chain health
- loyalty liability snapshot

This is not decorative reporting. It is **operational compression**: many fragmented surfaces reduced into one decision surface.

## Why It Matters

## 1. It reduces managerial blindness

Today, a supervisor must mentally stitch together meaning from multiple screens. That creates:

- slow shift handoff
- transcription errors
- inconsistent interpretation
- missed exceptions
- dependence on individual memory and competence

The reporting layer replaces fragmented observation with a single, structured operating picture.

## 2. It turns features into business proof

PT-2 already has meaningful architectural advantages:

- transparent theo computation
- immutable financial records
- append-only loyalty ledger
- enforced staff attribution
- structured audit lineage
- anomaly detection with acknowledgment lifecycle

Those are powerful capabilities, but without reporting they remain internal system virtues.

The reporting layer is what makes those virtues **visible, legible, and sellable**.

It is the commercial conversion point between:
- “the system has strong internals”
and
- “management can rely on this system as an operating authority.”

## 3. It creates action, not just observation

A useful report does not merely show numbers. It highlights what demands intervention.

That means the report should answer questions such as:

- Which tables materially deviated from baseline?
- Which alerts remain unacknowledged?
- Which shifts had weak rating coverage?
- Which tables opened on weaker provenance?
- Which compliance triggers occurred today?
- Which patterns may warrant managerial follow-up?

This is where PT-2 stops being a passive recordkeeper and starts functioning as an active operational intelligence layer.

## 4. It shortens the path to audit and compliance proof

The system already stores traceable inputs, actor attribution, and downstream linkage between rating, financial, compliance, and loyalty events.

The reporting layer exposes that structure in human-usable form.

That matters because “the data exists in the database” is not a serious answer in front of:

- a GM
- a finance stakeholder
- an internal auditor
- a regulator
- a prospective buyer evaluating trustworthiness

A report is the consumable proof surface.

## The Strongest Product Framing

Do **not** frame this as:
> “PT-2 has reports.”

That is weak and commoditized.

Frame it as:

> **PT-2 produces management-grade operational proof.**

Or:

> **PT-2 turns floor telemetry into a shift-ready operating brief and an audit-ready evidence package.**

Or:

> **PT-2 does not merely show what happened. It shows what mattered, what was abnormal, what can be trusted, and what can be proven.**

## The Five Questions the Reporting Layer Must Answer

Every serious gaming-day report should answer these five questions:

### 1. What did the floor actually produce?
Revenue-adjacent operating totals:
- drop
- fills
- credits
- win/loss
- hold
- theo

### 2. How complete and trustworthy is that picture?
Evidence-quality signals:
- rating coverage
- unrated / ghost exposure
- opening baseline provenance
- audit correlation completeness

### 3. What requires attention now?
Intervention signals:
- open anomalies
- unresolved alerts
- abnormal table behavior
- compliance events
- weak coverage zones

### 4. Who acted?
Accountability signals:
- staff attribution
- acknowledgments
- exception notes
- action chain visibility

### 5. Can this be defended upstream?
Management and control signals:
- end-of-shift handoff
- GM review
- finance reconciliation
- audit inspection
- regulatory inquiry

## Tangible Business Value

The reporting layer yields concrete value across four dimensions.

### Operational value
- faster shift close and handoff
- less manual stitching across screens
- fewer transcription mistakes
- quicker identification of problem tables
- better management focus

### Financial value
- faster recognition of abnormal drop / hold / win-loss patterns
- quicker intervention where leakage or operational failure may exist
- better visibility into rated vs unrated exposure
- better comprehension of loyalty liability

### Control value
- stronger exception tracking
- better accountability for acknowledged vs unresolved events
- clearer provenance behind opening bankroll assumptions
- less dependence on trust and memory

### Commercial value
- easier product demonstration
- stronger answer to “why is this system better than legacy?”
- clearer articulation of differentiated value
- more credible sales posture around proof, trust, and operating intelligence
